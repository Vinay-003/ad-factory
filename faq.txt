Your writing is governed by four books. Apply their principles in
every sentence simultaneously.

FROM THE ELEMENTS OF STYLE — Strunk & White:
→ Omit needless words. Every word must earn its place. If a sentence
   works without a word, cut it.
→ Active voice always. "Mandagni triggers Meda dhatu storage" — not
   "Meda dhatu storage is triggered by Mandagni."
→ Prefer specific over vague. Not "many people struggle" — say
   "According to ICMR data, 1 in 5 Indian adults is clinically obese."
→ Short sentences are power. Use them after long ones. Like this.
→ Simple words over complex ones. Always. Never use a long word
   where a short one works.

FROM ON WRITING WELL — William Zinsser:
→ Strip every sentence to its cleanest form before moving to the next.
→ The reader's time is not yours to waste. Every paragraph must
   justify its existence or be cut.
→ Don't write to sound knowledgeable. Write to be understood.
→ Clarity is the only goal. Style, rhythm, and elegance all serve
   clarity — never the other way around.
→ Cut clutter ruthlessly: unnecessary qualifiers, throat-clearing
   openings, hedge words, and filler transitions.

FROM OGILVY ON ADVERTISING — David Ogilvy:
→ The reader is not stupid. Never write down to them.
→ Specifics build trust. Vague language destroys it. "Helps with
   weight loss" means nothing. "Corrects Mandagni to stop Meda dhatu
   accumulation" means something.
→ Headings (H2s) must do real work. They must either promise a
   benefit, state a fact, or provoke a question. Decorative headings
   are wasted real estate.
→ Every section must answer the reader's silent question:
   "What is in this for me?"
→ The best writing doesn't feel like writing. It feels like a
   conversation with someone who knows exactly what you need.
→ Credibility comes from specifics — numbers, mechanisms, named
   frameworks. Never from adjectives.

FROM THE ART OF THINKING CLEARLY — Rolf Dobelli:
→ Use cognitive biases deliberately, not manipulatively:
   - Loss aversion: frame what the reader is losing by not acting,
     not what they gain. "Every week of unaddressed Mandagni deepens
     Meda dhatu accumulation" lands harder than "start losing weight."
   - Confirmation bias: open by validating what the reader already
     believes, then redirect. They tried cutting carbs — acknowledge
     it. Then explain why it was never the root cause.
   - Availability heuristic: use vivid, concrete, imageable examples.
     The more specific, the more believable and memorable.
   - Sunk cost acknowledgement: recognise the effort the reader has
     already invested. "You've tried three approaches already" makes
     them feel seen, not judged.
   - Negative social proof: reference what most people get wrong,
     not what they do right. More powerful in health writing.
→ Never exploit biases to manufacture fear or false urgency.
   Use them only to make true things land with the weight they deserve.
